Font Types...what they are and how to use them.

Typography plays a key role in creating quality websites, apps, emails, eBooks, and more. It involves creating or selecting a system of typefaces and fonts to express the written word. This creative art form can evoke strong emotions and helps express a brand's personality.

Well-crafted typography can enhance clear communication, legibility, and readability.

Below each font type sketch on this page are links to the fonts that were used in the descriptive examples.

Serif Fonts:

Google Fonts_Garamond

These are the oldest types of fonts, with the first examples appearing as early as the late 15th century. The word 'serif' refers to the small feet present at the tops and bottoms of each letter. These tiny flourishes stemmed from artists' brushes and would be added to the letters as decorative elements.

Today serif fonts are among the most popular typefaces in use and are considered to be very readable. They are often used in longer printed texts such as books, magazines, documents, and even logos, with styles like 'Times New Roman' being ever-present.

Some of the well-known serif fonts include Georgia, Times New Roman, Palatino, and Garamond.

Sans Serif Fonts:

Google Fonts_Open Sans

This simple, modern typeface does not include the flourishes found in serif typefaces. Offering a clean, modern, and minimalist aesthetic, it is highly legible on digital screens. Popular for web design, branding, and user interfaces, Open Sans is recognized as one of the most readable fonts and often considered to be one of the best fonts for websites.

Sans serif typeface examples include Helvetica, Roboto, Open Sans, and Montserrat.

Slab Serif Fonts:

Google Fonts_Roboto Slab

A slab serif font, also known as an Egyptian serif or just "slab", is a distinct typeface style characterized by bold, block-like serifs at the ends of strokes. Despite the name, slab serifs have no direct connection to Egypt, but instead reflect a timeless strength that made them ideal for large and impactful displays in posters and advertisements.

Slab serif fonts gained popularity during the early 19th century, particularly for advertising and print media, thanks to their ability to grab attention and convey strength. In the digital age, slab serif fonts evolved, balancing their historical charm with modern design requirements, making them a preferred choice in branding, editorial layouts, and user interfaces.

Though it's legible in small sizes, it's best suited for headlines, logos, and other larger design elements. Top slab serif fonts include Rockwell, Clarendon, and Roboto Slab.

Script Fonts:

Google Fonts_Pacifico

The origins of script fonts date back to classical calligraphy among the hand-lettered manuscripts from Roman and Renaissance times. This fluid, cursive typeface can range from elegant calligraphy to playful handwriting styles. Not ideal for body text, a script typeface works best for quotes, signatures, and short headers.

Examples include Snell Roundhand, Scriptina, and Pacifico.

Display Fonts:

Google Fonts_Abril Fatface

Also known as decorative, this attention-grabbing typeface is engineered for large-scale, high-impact usage. Its primary role is to immediately attract attention and help brands express their identity and create strong visual impressions. They convey a specific mood or tone, from vintage aesthetics to modern minimalism.

These expressive, often unique, and stylized fonts are designed to capture immediate attention and set a specific mood or aesthetic. Display typeface examples include Abril Fatface and pairing it with Lato for body text.

Monospace Fonts:

Google Fonts_Source Code Pro

This retro, typewriter-style typeface displays fixed-width letters and spacing, so that each character takes up the same width. Used in source code for easier readability, monospace typefaces are also used across numerous websites and graphic designs.

Monospace fonts invoke a retro, tech-oriented vibe and are ideally suited for programming interfaces or something like a vintage video game website. Examples include Courier, Bergen Mono, and Source Code Pro.

Ultimately, typography is not just about choosing a font; it is about organizing visual information to make the reader's experience enjoyable and effective.
